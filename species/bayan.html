Bayan

The Banyan Tree

A tree of extraordinary presence — the bayan spreads its canopy wide through a network of aerial roots that descend from branches and anchor into the earth, forming secondary trunks over time. In Cyprus it is planted as an ornamental and shade tree in parks, plazas, and historic gardens, where its vast canopy creates a natural gathering place during the island's long hot summers.

Identification

The bayan is immediately recognisable by its large, leathery, oval leaves with a glossy dark green surface, typically 15–30cm long. The most distinctive feature is its aerial prop roots — thick rope-like structures hanging from horizontal branches that eventually thicken into secondary trunks. The bark is pale grey and smooth on younger stems, roughening with age. Small red-orange fig fruits appear in pairs at leaf axils, attracting birds.

In Cyprus

The bayan is not native to Cyprus — it originates from the Indian subcontinent — but has been planted extensively as an ornamental tree. It thrives in Cyprus's warm Mediterranean climate and is commonly found in the grounds of government buildings, schools, churches, and public parks in towns such as Nicosia, Limassol, and Paphos. Some specimens in older town centres have been growing for over a century and have become local landmarks, with their sprawling roots and canopies dominating entire plazas.

Ecology & Growth

Bayan trees are members of the fig family and expand laterally through their aerial root system, allowing a single tree to colonise a considerable area over decades. In Cyprus the Mediterranean climate suits it well — it tolerates dry summers once established and grows vigorously in deep, well-watered soils. The dense canopy provides shade and the figs attract a wide range of birds and small wildlife. It is frost-sensitive and confined to lower elevations on the island.

Cultural Significance

Across its native range in India and Southeast Asia, the bayan is considered sacred in Hinduism and Buddhism — a symbol of wisdom, longevity, and shelter. In Cyprus it carries a different kind of significance: the shade of a large bayan in a town square is a natural meeting point for coffee, conversation, and rest during the heat of the day. Several notable specimens in Nicosia's old city have been informally protected by local municipalities as heritage trees.

Conservation Status

As an introduced ornamental species in Cyprus, the bayan faces no conservation concerns on the island. Globally the species is classified as Least Concern. The main threat to established specimens in Cyprus is urban development and infrastructure works that can damage root systems. Older landmark trees are increasingly recognised for their amenity and heritage value.

Where to See It

Notable bayan specimens can be found in Nicosia's Ataturk Square area and in the grounds of several older schools and government buildings across the island. The larger parks in Limassol and Paphos also contain fine examples. When identifying, look for the characteristic hanging aerial roots even on young branches — they are the bayan's unmistakable calling card.
