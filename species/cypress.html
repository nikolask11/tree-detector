Cupressus sempervirens · Cupressaceae

Cypress

Mediterranean Cypress · Italian Cypress

The Mediterranean cypress and Cyprus share more than geographic proximity — the very word "cypress" derives from the Greek name for this island. Cupressus sempervirens defines the mountain skylines of the Kyrenia range and appears throughout the island's history, mythology, and landscape. Its dark, flame-like silhouette is one of the most instantly recognisable forms in the Mediterranean world.

Identification

Cupressus sempervirens is identified by its strongly columnar to fastigiate (narrowly upright) form — the classic pencil or flame shape — dark green scale-like leaves tightly pressed to the branches, and rounded grey-brown cones of 2–4cm that persist on the tree for several years. The bark is grey-brown, fibrous, and furrowed in mature specimens. The foliage is aromatic when crushed, with a resinous, slightly sharp scent. Some wild specimens develop broader, more irregular crowns at maturity.

In Cyprus

The Mediterranean cypress is native to Cyprus and is most extensively found along the Kyrenia (Pentadaktylos) mountain range in the north, where it forms pure stands on steep limestone slopes and mixed forests with Pinus brutia. Smaller cypress forests and scattered trees occur across the island from 100 to 1,200m altitude, including in the Lemesos Forest, near Koili village in Paphos, and in gorges of the Akamas peninsula. The species is also widely planted as an ornamental around churches, cemeteries, and historic properties throughout Cyprus.

Name & Etymology

The word "cypress" derives from the Latin cypressus, which came from the Greek kyparissos. One ancient explanation links it directly to the island of Kypros (Cyprus), where the tree was abundant and culturally important. Another tradition derives the name from Cyparissus, a youth in Greek mythology who was transformed into a cypress tree by Apollo after accidentally killing his beloved stag — explaining the cypress's ancient association with mourning and loss. The name of the island itself may ultimately derive from the word for copper (kypros), which was smelted using Cypriot pine and cypress wood in antiquity.

Symbolism & Culture

The cypress has been a symbol of mourning, eternity, and the afterlife across Mediterranean cultures since antiquity, and this tradition continues in Cyprus today — cypress trees are commonly planted in Orthodox Christian cemeteries. The tree's evergreen nature and extraordinary longevity made it a natural symbol of immortality. In ancient Persian garden design, the cypress represented the tree of life. In Cyprus, cypress timber was historically valued for its durability, rot resistance, and aromatic properties, used in furniture, chests, and sacred objects.

Ecology

Cupressus sempervirens on Cyprus grows predominantly on limestone and marl substrates, where it often colonises steep, rocky slopes that are too difficult for other trees. It is drought-tolerant and fire-resilient, and its dense columnar form limits the spread of surface fires between individual trees. The Pentadaktylos cypress forests represent a distinct forest habitat type (EU Annex I habitat 9290) of conservation significance. These forests support a range of endemic and specialist species in their understorey.

Where to See It

The most dramatic cypress forests are found along the Kyrenia range — visible from a great distance as dark columnar trees on the pale limestone ridges. The drive along the north coast road offers panoramic views of cypress-clad hillsides. In the south, cypresses appear around the monasteries of Troodos, particularly Kykkos and Trooditissa, where old planted specimens line the approaches. Every traditional village cemetery on the island will have at least one sentinel cypress marking its entrance.
