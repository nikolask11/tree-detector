Eucalyptus camaldulensis · Myrtaceae

Eucalyptus

River Red Gum

Introduced to Cyprus in the late 19th century as part of drainage and afforestation schemes, the eucalyptus has become one of the most widespread and immediately recognisable trees on the island. Its pale, peeling bark, silvery-blue leaves, and menthol fragrance make it unmistakable, lining roadsides, riverbanks, and agricultural margins across the lowlands.

Identification

Eucalyptus is identified by its long, lance-shaped leaves that hang vertically and appear grey-green or blue-green with a waxy coating. The bark sheds in long strips to reveal smooth surfaces ranging from white to grey and brown beneath. Creamy white flowers in small clusters appear seasonally. The familiar medicinal menthol scent is strongest when leaves are crushed.

In Cyprus

Eucalyptus trees were introduced by British colonial administrators in the late 1800s, primarily to drain marshy lowland areas and reduce mosquito breeding grounds. Their extraordinary water uptake made them effective for this purpose. They were also planted along roadsides and around farmland for shade and windbreak. Today they grow across the entire island from sea level up to around 600m, predominantly in the Mesaoria plain and coastal lowlands.

Ecology & Growth

One of the fastest-growing trees in the world, eucalyptus can reach significant heights within a decade. In Cyprus it grows particularly well along seasonal streams and irrigation channels. Its deep root system and high water consumption can lower local water tables — useful for drainage but sometimes problematic in drier areas. The tree also releases chemicals through leaf litter that suppress the growth of other plants beneath it.

Uses

In Cyprus, eucalyptus timber has historically been used for fence posts, tool handles, and fuel wood. Eucalyptus oil is used in folk medicine as a remedy for respiratory ailments. The flowers are an important late-season nectar source for honeybees, and the trees serve as effective windbreaks around citrus orchards.

Conservation Notes

Eucalyptus is non-native and there is ongoing debate about its impact on local biodiversity. Its dense leaf litter alters soil chemistry in ways that can reduce native understory plants. Cyprus's Department of Forests has shifted towards native species in new reforestation projects, though established groves are retained where they provide practical benefits.

Where to See It

Found almost everywhere on Cyprus below 600m. Look for it lining the banks of the Pedieos and Kouris rivers, along rural roadsides throughout the Mesaoria plain, and around agricultural land. The white peeling bark and hanging grey-green leaves make it easy to identify from a distance.
