Olive

The Common Olive

Cultivated on Cyprus for more than two thousand years, the olive tree is the island's most culturally resonant tree. Its gnarled silver-grey trunks, shimmering leaves, and golden fruit are inseparable from the Cypriot identity — woven into cuisine, folklore, ceremony, and landscape in equal measure. Where the sun shines and love exists, say the Cypriots, there you will find olive trees.

Identification

The olive is a small to medium evergreen tree with a characteristically gnarled and twisted trunk that develops deep furrows with age. Leaves are narrow, lance-shaped, dark green above and silvery-grey beneath — giving groves their characteristic shimmering quality in the breeze. Small, fragrant white flowers appear in late spring. The fruit begins green, ripening through yellow to deep purple-black in autumn. Ancient specimens develop enormous, hollow, fissured trunks of extraordinary character.

In Cyprus

The olive has been cultivated in Cyprus since at least the Bronze Age, with archaeological evidence of olive oil production dating back 3,500 years. It is found island-wide, from lowland agricultural areas to hillside terraces up to around 800m altitude. Wild olive trees (Olea europaea var. sylvestris) grow naturally in the maquis shrubland of the dry lowlands, often alongside carob and Phoenician juniper. Cultivated groves, many of them centuries old, dominate the hillsides around Paphos, Limassol, and the Troodos foothills.

Cultural Significance

In Cyprus, the olive carries deep symbolic weight. It is a symbol of immortality — the tree survives fire, drought, and even cutting, regenerating from the stump. Superstitious tradition holds that olive branches ward off the evil eye and evil spirits, and olive wood or oil features in blessings for weddings, births, and the opening of new ventures. The Cypriot saying "Where is the sun and love, there are olive trees" captures the tree's place at the heart of island life. An olive branch has been the universal symbol of peace since Old Testament times.

Olive Oil in Cyprus

Olive oil is a cornerstone of Cypriot cuisine and one of the island's major agricultural exports. Cyprus produces predominantly extra virgin olive oil from varieties including Ladoelia, Kolymbada, and Kothreiki. The harvest runs from October to December, when families and small producers gather olives by hand or with mechanical harvesters. Cold pressing at local co-operative mills preserves the oil's flavour. Cypriot olive oil has a rich, fruity character and is used extensively in meze, bread dipping, and traditional dishes.

Ecology

The olive is superbly adapted to the Mediterranean climate — tolerating drought, poor rocky soils, and intense heat. It grows slowly but lives extraordinarily long; some trees in Cyprus are estimated to be several hundred years old. The tree provides habitat for insects, birds, and small mammals. Old olive groves with their complex trunk structures and dense understorey are recognised as important biodiversity habitats across the Mediterranean.

Where to See It

Olive groves are ubiquitous across the Cypriot countryside. The hillsides around Paphos, the Troodos foothills near Platres and Omodos, and the terraced landscapes of the Pitsilia region contain particularly beautiful old groves. The village of Omodos in the Limassol district is especially known for its ancient olive trees, some with trunks of extraordinary girth.
