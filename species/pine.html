Pinus brutia · Pinaceae

Pine

Calabrian Pine · Turkish Pine

The Calabrian pine is the dominant forest tree of Cyprus, covering both the Troodos and Kyrenia mountain ranges from sea level to approximately 1,400 metres. Its reddish-brown furrowed bark, long paired needles, and heavy cones are defining features of the Cypriot mountain landscape. The entire island's modern forest cover owes much to reforestation efforts using this species.

Identification

Pinus brutia is identified by its paired, stiff, slightly twisted needles of 10–16cm in length, its stout oval cones of 5–10cm that persist on branches for several years, and its distinctive reddish-orange to grey-brown deeply furrowed bark on mature trunks. Young trees have a conical form; mature specimens develop broad, rounded or irregular crowns. The resinous scent of pine is strong and characteristic, particularly after rain.

In Cyprus

Pinus brutia forms the most extensive forests on Cyprus, covering both the Troodos massif and the Kyrenia range. It is the backbone of the island's forest cover and the primary species used in the landmark reforestation programme initiated in the early 20th century — a programme famously supported by Winston Churchill as Under-Secretary for the Colonies in 1907, after centuries of deforestation for copper smelting had stripped the mountains bare. Today's forests are largely the result of that planting effort and subsequent decades of work by the Cyprus Forestry Department.

Ecology

The Calabrian pine is well adapted to the hot, dry Mediterranean summers of Cyprus. It is fire-resilient to a degree — its thick bark offers some protection and it regenerates vigorously after fire — but large wildfires remain a serious threat to Cyprus's pine forests, as seen in major fires in 1995 and more recently. The understorey beneath pine forests on Cyprus is rich with shrubs including mastic, strawberry tree, Jerusalem sage, and the endemic golden oak.

Pine Honey

Cyprus is famed for its pine honey — one of the island's most prized and exported food products. This honey is not made from pine flowers but from the honeydew secreted by the scale insect Marchalina hellenica, which feeds on the sap of Pinus brutia. Bees collect this sticky secretion and convert it into a dark, richly aromatic honey with a distinctive resinous flavour. Troodos pine honey is considered among the finest in the Mediterranean and carries protected designation of origin status.

Uses & Timber

Historically, Cypriot pine timber was extensively used in construction, shipbuilding, and — most significantly — as fuel for the island's ancient copper smelters, which gave Cyprus its very name (Kypros, derived from kypros, the word for copper). Today the timber is used for construction, carpentry, and firewood. Resin tapping from pine trees was once practised in Cyprus for the production of turpentine and pitch.

Where to See It

Pine forest is encountered throughout the Troodos mountains — the drive from Limassol to Platres and Troodos village passes through extensive stands. The Kyrenia range in the north is also largely forested with pine and cypress. The Caledonian Falls trail and Cedar Valley in the Troodos offer particularly beautiful pine forest walks.
